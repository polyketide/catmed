# Feline Upper-Airway Tumours: Validity of Response Markers, and the Recovery Kinetics of Voice Loss and Nictitating Membrane Protrusion

> **Generated**: 2026-07-19　**Method**: parallel literature search across 6 domains + point-by-point adversarial verification (36 agents)
> **Verification result**: of 30 load-bearing conclusions, **16 were judged OVERSTATED** (only 14 CONFIRMED). What this document records is the **post-verification** wording.
> **Stance**: the primary function of this document is **negative** — to set out which markers cannot be used and which numbers do not exist. Wherever the text says "not found", that is a genuine evidence gap, and **any number filling that gap is fabricated**.

---

## 0. The whole thing in one sentence

**Neither voice loss nor nictitating membrane protrusion is a response marker; "brighter demeanour" is the most misleading of the lot; whether antineoplastic treatment is working can only be judged by re-imaging and measuring the mass.**

---

## 1. Vocalisation (voice loss / dysphonia)

### 1.1 Epidemiology
| Data | Source |
|---|---|
| 23 cats with primary laryngeal/tracheal lymphoma: dysphonia **5/23 = 22%** (increased respiratory effort 74% and abnormal upper respiratory tract sounds 48% were more common) | PMID 36655881, J Feline Med Surg 2023 |
| 18 cats with tracheal mass lesions (15 lymphoma): voice change **2/18 = 11%** (⚠️ corrected 2026-07-19 against the PMC full text: the 28% originally recorded here is in fact the figure for **coughing** — the source reads "coughing (n = 5; 28%) and voice change (n = 2; 11%)"); median survival 214 days in the lymphoma group vs 21 days for other tumour types | PMID 37199684 |
| PLTL: mean duration of clinical signs before presentation **57.5 days (range 2–515)** | PMID 36655881 |

⚠️ The 11–22% range comes from a **selected population with already-confirmed laryngeal/tracheal tumours**, and **cannot be inverted** into "a cat that loses its voice has a 22% chance of having a tumour".
⚠️ **No study in the literature reports the frequency of dysphonia as a *presenting* sign** — a genuine gap.

### 1.2 Time course of recovery: the feline data are zero
- **There exists no** case report documenting recovery of vocalisation after treatment in a cat, nor its time course. **The number "usually recovers in a few weeks" does not exist.**
- The feline laryngeal paralysis literature (Hardie, 10 cats, PMID 19538664; Thunberg, 14 cats, PMID 21041335) **uses airway outcomes as the endpoint throughout; not one records postoperative vocalisation**.
- The only directly citable feline natural history: an 8-week-old cat developed left laryngeal paralysis after PDA ligation and **still had persistent arytenoid dysfunction 7 months postoperatively**, judged permanent (PMID 23725437, JAVMA 2013).

**Time anchors available only by extrapolation from human data:**
| Cohort | Recovery rate | Median time |
|---|---|---|
| Vocal cord paralysis after minimally invasive McKeown oesophagectomy, n=90 | cumulative **65.4%** at 7 months (left 68.6% / right 55.6% / **bilateral only 20.0%**) | **183 days** |
| Left recurrent laryngeal nerve paralysis after oesophagectomy, n=85 | **25.8%** | — |

The two cohorts differ enormously (65.4% vs 25.8%) → **no reliable recovery-probability figure exists**. But both use **6 months** as the threshold for declaring permanence, and that anchor is consistent.

### 1.3 Mechanism: compression is reversible, infiltration is not — but "chronic" compression is a separate matter
- Early compression is predominantly **demyelinating** (rat chronic compression model, PMID 15144876) → reversible.
- But after **prolonged chronic compression, decompression "generally improves sensation but rarely reverses motor atrophy"** (PMID 29257018, JBJS 2017), attributed to persistent glial and perineurial scarring.
- Fifteen months after recurrent laryngeal nerve transection and ligation in dogs, **7 of the 8 dogs surviving the period showed anatomical regeneration** — but the clinical picture was synkinesis, and **the vocal fold still did not move** (PMID 6817274). **Anatomical regeneration ≠ functional recovery.**

> **∴ Where voice loss has already persisted for months, the prior probability of motor recovery is low even if the tumour shrinks and the compression is relieved.**
> **∴ "Other signs improving with zero change in vocalisation" is an internally consistent picture, and does not require assuming that the antineoplastic treatment has failed.**

### 1.4 Vocalisation is "insensitive" to laryngeal function — the key evidence in the opposite direction
Among 138 dogs presented for cough alone (without signs of upper airway disease), 26 (19%) had laryngeal paresis/paralysis, yet **only 2 had dysphonia and 1 had stridor** (PMID 27379595, JAVMA 2016).
→ **Laryngeal function can be very poor while the voice sounds normal. Conversely, failure of the voice to return does not quantitatively reflect either the extent of disease or the response to treatment.**

### 1.5 The literature's own repudiation of using signs to judge response
The authors of the PLTL study write in their limitations that the response rate in their study **should be interpreted with caution, because it is based on clinical signs rather than on objective, measurable response assessment**, and they recommend that "systematic restaging should always be the gold standard" (PMID 36655881, Discussion).
⚠️ All 5 dysphonic cats in that study were recorded as CR — but the study **defines CR as "resolution of all clinical signs"**, which is **circular**, and it cannot be used to promise an owner that the voice will come back.

## 2. Nictitating membrane (third eyelid) protrusion

### 2.1 Core conclusion: improvement of the nictitating membrane is **not** evidence of tumour shrinkage

Three independent lines of evidence:

1. **Mechanism**: the feline nictitating membrane is maintained by smooth muscle alone, and requires only a "relatively small amount of sympathetic activity" to stay retracted (Paton 1970, PMID 5498516) → the threshold is very low and it is highly sensitive to systemic state. **Resolution of fever, improved hydration, or reduced pain are each sufficient on their own to let it return to position.**
2. **Analogy (corrected 2026-07-19 after retrieving the full text — the 46–68% and 25% originally written here do not appear in the source)**: in 44 dogs with extra-axial brain tumours, of the 34 given steroids, **21 (62%) had subjective clinical improvement**; whereas peritumoral oedema volume actually decreased by >30% in only **17/44 (39%)**. The authors' own words: *"subjective improvement of clinical signs can be seen without significant decrease to the VPTE on imaging"* and *"the subjective clinical improvement with steroids was not systematically associated with a decreased of PTBE and some dogs had clinical improvement despite increased PTBE"* (PMID 39552196; PMC full text retrieved and checked).
3. **Confounding**: among cats with suppurative otitis media, Horner's syndrome occurred in **7/26 (27%)** (PMID 39344808) — a condition in which **54% had bilateral involvement** and **19/26 (73%) were clinically healed 60–240 days after myringotomy plus medical treatment**, so this is a confounder with a good prognosis rather than an ominous one.
   ⚠️ **One cat in that series "died a few hours after the procedure."** n=1, and the authors' own conclusion is that the technique is "practical, safe and efficient" — so this is not evidence against the procedure. It is recorded because this knowledge base tracks the cost of intervention as carefully as the cost of inaction (see `emergency-triage-red-flags.md` §3), and a feline peri-procedural death belongs in that ledger even when the series concludes the procedure is safe. Obstruction of the auditory tube by a mass will almost inevitably bring secondary middle ear inflammation — and **antibiotics plus steroids are precisely the standard treatment for that inflammation**, which is enough to explain improvement of the nictitating membrane on its own, **without ever invoking the hypothesis of tumour shrinkage**.

⚠️ **On the question of whether nictitating membrane protrusion fluctuates with systemic condition, the search returned nothing.** No figure of the form "there is an X% chance this is only systemic improvement" can be given. The honest statement is: **the two explanations cannot be distinguished on the current evidence.**
⚠️ The textbook pathway of "dehydration/emaciation → loss of the orbital fat pad → nictitating membrane protrusion" has **no quantitative clinical evidence in cats** that could be found.

### 2.2 The prior question: is this actually Horner's?
**Miosis is the core diagnostic sign of Horner's syndrome; protrusion of the nictitating membrane is only an accompanying sign** (van den Broek 1987, J Small Anim Pract; ⚠️ mixed canine/feline, not stratified by species, PMID not obtained — have the veterinarian check the original before relying on it).

Three things must be checked, because **the three pathways carry sharply different clinical meanings**:

| Finding | Points to | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Unilateral + ipsilateral miosis + ptosis + **enophthalmos** | Horner's | Sympathetic pathway involved |
| **Bilateral + no miosis** | Low sympathetic tone / Haw's-like | **Unrelated to tumour volume**; Haw's resolved in 9 of 10 cats within 37.7 ± 23 (12–95) days, though it recurred in 3 of those 9 at 156–182 days (PMID 39267365) |
| **Exophthalmos** rather than enophthalmos | Mass effect | In a retrobulbar tumour series, 28% had nictitating membrane protrusion (exophthalmos was the commonest sign at 84%) (PMID 11397315); here, and only here, improvement may genuinely reflect a reduction in mass effect |

### 2.3 ⭐ The single most actionable item: the 1% phenylephrine eye-drop test
- **Zero risk, at the bedside, result within minutes**, and it answers both "is this Horner's?" and "preganglionic or postganglionic?": **mydriasis within 20 minutes = postganglionic; 20–45 minutes = preganglionic** (PMID 24028692; ⚠️ these thresholds come from **idiopathic canine** data).
- For a cat with almost no airway reserve, this is **one of the very few investigations that adds essentially no procedural risk**.
- ⚠️ **The window closes**: once the protrusion has largely resolved and denervation supersensitivity has waned, the test becomes uninterpretable. **It should be done as soon as possible while any asymmetry remains.**

### 2.4 ⚠️ An anatomical inconsistency (important, do not skip)
Validated feline anatomy places the sympathetic fibres in the tympanic plexus and jugular foramen at the **roof** of the bulla (PMID 3768681), whereas the CT report describes the lesion extending to the **ventral** aspect of the tympanic cavity.
→ **The statement "the mass invades the ventral tympanic cavity" is not by itself sufficient to infer sympathetic pathway involvement.** The true point of involvement is more likely in the retropharyngeal space or the jugular foramen region. **This can only be settled by reading the original CT/MRI; it cannot be inferred from a case summary.**

### 2.5 The voice loss and the nictitating membrane are quite likely two signs of one lesion
A closely comparable feline case was found: a **7-year-old neutered male cat with Horner's syndrome + voice change + dysphagia + third eyelid protrusion**; laryngoscopy showed left laryngeal paralysis; CT showed a mass extending beyond the tympanic bulla with osteolysis; histopathology gave a **middle ear adenocarcinoma** invading CN IX/X/XI/XII and the sympathetic trunk via the jugular foramen and hypoglossal canal (**Villaret's syndrome**) (PMID 37576831, Front Vet Sci 2023).

⚠️ **Diagnostic implication**: in the literature this phenotype corresponds to **undifferentiated carcinoma / adenocarcinoma rather than lymphoma**. **A clinical response to steroids and asparaginase cannot be used to rule out carcinoma.**
⚠️ But this is an **n=1 case report**, usable only to generate a hypothesis, **not to estimate incidence, time course or prognosis**.

### 2.6 Why "the nictitating membrane resolved but the voice is still completely gone" is not a contradiction
Postganglionic sympathetic fibres are **unmyelinated C fibres**, sensitive to mild compression, and recovery from neurapraxia is measured in **days to weeks**; whereas voice loss persisting for months, if it arises from injury to the recurrent laryngeal / vagal **motor axons**, recovers over **months** or not at all. **The two recovery rates differ by an order of magnitude to begin with.**

> **The conclusion holds symmetrically: recovery of the nictitating membrane does not prove the tumour shrank, and failure of the voice to recover does not prove it did not.**

## 3. Validity ranking of at-home markers (all are borrowed; not one has been validated in cats with upper-airway tumours)

### 3.1 Stridor — usable, but only as an **ordinal** scale, and it is **asymmetric**
From canine BOAS data: the **specificity of laryngeal stridor for laryngeal collapse was 100%** (both pre- and post-exercise), while the **sensitivity of clinical examination for BOAS diagnosis was only 56.7% pre-exercise**, rising to 70% after a 5-minute walk test and 93.3% after a 3-minute trot test (PMID 30666670).

> **∴ "Stridor now present even at rest" is almost certainly genuine deterioration (high specificity).**
> **∴ "No stridor" is no reassurance at all (low sensitivity).**

🚫 **Safety red line**: canine BOAS work raises sensitivity by means of a **standardised exercise test** — **this could be fatal in a cat with near-zero airway reserve and a history of respiratory arrest, and an exercise load must never be applied deliberately.** One may only **passively make use of** the cat's own spontaneous eating and grooming; it must not be provoked.

### 3.2 Resting/sleeping respiratory rate (SRR) — usable, but the method must change
- **Use the cat's own baseline, not a fixed cut-off of >30.**
- ⭐ **The inspiratory-phase duration must be watched at the same time**: human data show that **the physiological signature of fixed upper-airway obstruction is a rise in Ti/Ttot (0.42 vs 0.37) and a fall in peak inspiratory flow, not a rise in rate** (PMID 2376249). **Watching rate alone will miss it.**
- A respiratory rate measured in the consulting room is **worthless** for these cats: in clinically healthy adult cats it ranged 28–176 breaths/min (median 64), with a calculated reference interval of 32–135 (PMID 29680402).

### 3.3 ⚠️ "Brighter demeanour" — **the most misleading** marker of them all
The canine intracranial tumour data show plainly that under steroids **"subjective improvement of clinical signs can be seen without significant decrease to the VPTE on imaging"** (PMID 39552196). While a cat is on prednisolone, improved demeanour **carries almost no information about tumour burden**.

### 3.4 Statement of gaps
The validity of SRR in cats, the quantitative stridor-to-calibre relationship in cats, a respiratory-distress posture scoring scale for cats, and the relationship between grooming/eating duration and the airway in cats — **all four searches returned nothing**. The "methods" above are mechanistic inference, not validated instruments.

⚠️ The specific percentage thresholds from human CFD models (75%/90%) **should not be transferred directly to cats** (the feline larynx and nasopharynx are an order of magnitude smaller in absolute scale, so Reynolds numbers and the turbulent transition point differ). Only the qualitative conclusion — that **a steep threshold effect exists** — should be carried across.

## 4. Heat and activity: the risk during a period of improvement

### 4.1 Direct feline data
SAVSNET primary-care surveillance in the UK, 2013–2018, 174 heat-related illness (HRI) cases in total (146 dogs, **16 cats**, 8 guinea pigs, 3 rabbits, 1 ferret):
- **10/16 (62.5%) of the feline records documented no trigger**; among the **only 6 cases where a trigger was recorded**, all were **environmental** (PMID 35342739).
  ⚠️ Verification correction: this is **not** "16 cases, 100% environmental". The authors attribute the missing triggers to free-roaming and unwitnessed onset, and note that the literature **does contain rare reports of exertional HRI in cats after hunting in hot conditions**.
- **Seasonality: cases occurred May–September, with 12/16 (75.0%) concentrated in June and July** (6 each).
- **Age: median 10.4 years (range 0.4–19, IQR 4.6–14.2), and cats over 15 years made up 25% of feline cases** — significantly overrepresented against the VetCompass feline population (7.82%, *P* < 0.05). The authors attribute this to age-related decline in thermoregulation plus the higher prevalence of cardiac, respiratory and renal disease in old cats.
- **⭐ The presenting signs invert the canine pattern, and this is the part an owner would get wrong.** Of the 14 feline cases with signs recorded, **lethargy was commonest (10/14), ahead of abnormal breathing (6/14)**; in dogs the order is reversed — abnormal breathing 72/134, lethargy 55/134, collapse 46/134 (Table 4, PMID 35342739).
  ⚠️ **Why this matters for triage rather than for classification.** Heat illness in dogs is culturally encoded as a *breathing* emergency — panting, distress, collapse. In this dataset the commonest feline presentation is a cat that is simply flat. Combine that with the two findings above — no witnessed trigger in 62.5%, and a quarter of cases in cats over 15 — and the failure mode writes itself: **an old cat, indoors, no visible cause, "just quiet today".** An owner screening for laboured breathing sees nothing to act on.
  ⚠️ **Limits, stated plainly.** 16 cats, 14 with signs recorded. Counts this small carry wide uncertainty and the lethargy-over-breathing ordering could reverse in another sample. It is a **reason not to use breathing as the sole heat-illness screen in cats**, not a validated feline sign ranking. Retrospective free-text clinical narratives also under-record signs nobody thought to type.

### 4.2 Canine quantitative data (counterintuitive)
- Among exertional HRI cases, **372/551 (67.5%) followed nothing more than "a walk"**, with high-intensity activity accounting for only 17.6% (PMID 32751913) → **low-intensity activity is sufficient.** ✅ Verified against the publisher full text 2026-07-20.
  ⚠️ The category "a walk" has no equivalent exposure in cats, so **this proportional distribution is not transferable to them.**

**⭐ From the same paper's full text — a finding that weakens an argument made elsewhere in this project.** Among triggers for heat-related illness, **"undergoing treatment (vet/groomer)" accounted for 31 events (5.2% of known triggers), with 3 fatalities (9.7%)** — but the odds ratio for fatality was **1.3 (95% CI 0.37–4.51, p = 0.680)**, i.e. **not significantly different** from exertional HRI. For comparison, building confinement was significant at OR 6.06 (2.13–17.22, p = 0.001).

> **What this does and does not support.** It confirms that being handled at a veterinary practice or groomer is a *recorded trigger* of heat-related illness, and that such events do kill. It does **not** support the stronger claim that attending a clinic carries a distinctively elevated fatality risk — in this dataset it did not, and the confidence interval is wide enough to include no effect.
>
> ⚠️ **This project has used "transport is itself a lethal risk" to argue that over-triage has a real cost** (`docs/LITERATURE-PIPELINE-SOP.md`, `emergency-triage-red-flags.md` §3). That argument rested on a single canine case reaching 42.5 °C. The population data here are more equivocal, and the honest position is now: the mechanism is real and documented, the magnitude is not established, and it should not be quoted as though it were. **This is a dog dataset besides; no feline equivalent has been found.**
- On the days of HRI events, the **median ambient temperature was only 16.9 °C** (PMID 35622759) → **heat injury does not require extreme heat.**
  ⚠️ Note on wording: the abstract states "ambient temperature". An earlier draft of this document described this figure as WBGT (wet bulb globe temperature, a composite heat-stress index of air temperature, radiant heat, humidity and wind speed, and a daily peak rather than a mean); **the abstract does not support that characterisation** — treat it as ambient temperature unless the full text says otherwise.

### 4.3 ⭐ Transport is itself a lethal risk item
A **7-year-old neutered male dog with dysphonia and laryngeal obstruction** became severely cyanotic with a core temperature of **42.5 °C** from **the stress of transport plus the physical examination itself** (Burger 2018, Vlaams Diergeneeskd Tijdschr; ⚠️ not indexed in PubMed).
> **∴ The transport for every recheck, CT or biopsy must be entered into the decision as an independent lethal risk item, not treated as a neutral preliminary step.**

### 4.4 Mechanism: the vicious circle of heat
Heat dissipation depends on driving airflow across the upper respiratory tract, with the principal heat exchange occurring at the **nasal epithelium** (PMID 16675613) — and that is exactly the segment which is obstructed. Cats begin to recruit panting at **30 °C, and more than half are panting at 35 °C** (Amini-Sereshki 1988; ⚠️ borrowed control data from healthy laboratory cats).
⚠️ The mechanism is sound but **there is no direct empirical closure**; "increased activity after improvement precipitating a crisis" is **nowhere directly documented** in the literature. It is an **inference** synthesised from two verified facts, and must not be presented as "proven by the literature".

### 4.5 ⚠️ Steroid tapering: the peak of rebound laryngeal oedema is about **1 week after discontinuation**
In a rabbit model, the laryngeal lumen area in the group 1 week after discontinuation was significantly smaller than in controls (PMID 19123077).
> **This is a counterintuitive time point: the first few days after stopping, when things "look all right", are exactly when vigilance is most easily lost.**
⚠️ The rabbit model had n=6 per group, was not a tumour model, and its doses are not directly convertible; **it cannot be used as a precise tapering timetable for a cat.**

### 4.6 Statement of gaps
**No consensus document on home management of feline upper-airway obstruction was found; no veterinary publication giving a specific upper temperature limit was found; no evidence-based threshold for when to use an oxygen cage was found. Any specific temperature figure can only be labelled "derived from indirect data", and must never be presented as "guideline-recommended".**
The relative weighting between triggers (exercise vs excitement vs high temperature vs humidity vs stress) **does not exist in the literature** — do not invent a ranking.

## 5. L-asparaginase: time course and diagnostic information content

### 5.1 The assessment window is measured in **days**, not hours
Response assessment for L-Asp in cats was set at **7±2 days after administration** (actually recorded as 5–9 days) (Inazumi 2024, University of Tokyo, 43 cats with large-cell alimentary lymphoma).
⚠️ This is a time point **that study set for itself**; it is **not a "standard assessment window" established by any guideline or consensus**.
- With the **native formulation**, asparagine depletion **rebounds by day 7** (LeBlanc 2007) → **2 doses ≈ roughly 2 weeks of coverage.**

### 5.2 ⭐ The diagnostic value of "no shrinkage" depends on the **formulation**
| Formulation | Response rate in true lymphoma | Likelihood ratio of "no shrinkage" |
|---|---|---|
| **Native** (Leunase, common in Japan) | imaging **37.2%** (CR 7.0% + PR 30.2%) / LeBlanc 30% | **LR− ≈ 0.65 — barely shifts the posterior** |
| **Pegylated** | 74–82% | roughly **4–5×** evidence against |

> **∴ If the native formulation was used, "two doses and no shrinkage" is almost no evidence at all for "this is not lymphoma"** — because 63–70% of genuine lymphomas do not shrink either.
⚠️ The LR is an **arithmetic extrapolation** performed on the verified response rates, not a value reported in the literature, and it depends on the untested assumption that P(no shrinkage | carcinoma) ≈ 1.

### 5.3 Symptomatic improvement **cannot** distinguish lymphoma from carcinoma
- Feline **granulomatous laryngitis** is **clinically and radiographically indistinguishable from laryngeal tumours, and responds to steroids** (Tasker 1999).
- Prednisolone independently improves oedema, demeanour and nictitating membrane protrusion.
⚠️ Response rate and time to effect of prednisolone monotherapy in feline lymphoma: **no first-hand quantitative feline data were found, so no figure is given.**

### 5.4 ⭐ Steroids do **not** reduce the diagnostic yield of tissue sampling
From a large human series: pre-biopsy steroids did **not** reduce diagnostic yield (**83% vs 81%**); what actually determines success is **the sampling method** — **fine-needle aspiration had a 69% lower success rate than incisional biopsy (OR 0.31)**.
> **∴ Sampling should not be abandoned on the grounds that "the cat is already on prednisolone"; what should be fought for is a tissue (forceps) sample rather than a fine needle.**
⚠️ The quantitative effect of steroids on **PARR positivity rate**: **no direct study was found in cats, dogs or humans (unknown).** The mechanistic concern is reasonable but has not been quantified.

### 5.5 The current window of improvement has an expected length
Single-agent L-Asp in feline lymphoma gives a **median disease-free interval of about 70 days** (Bik 2023; ⚠️ PMID not obtained).
⚠️ This applies only to lymphoma. If the lesion is an undifferentiated carcinoma, the expected response to L-Asp is very low, and **the current "improvement" needs to be re-attributed** (most likely to the anti-oedema / anti-inflammatory effect of the steroid) — if so, **more is lost on tapering than expected, and the rebound risk is correspondingly higher.**

### 5.6 The boundary of cohort extrapolation
The Inazumi, Krupa and Bik cohorts consist mainly of **alimentary/multicentric** lymphoma; **response rates and time courses for the nasopharyngeal/laryngeal subgroup are not reported separately**. The pharynx and larynx are bounded by bone and cartilage, so **volume change is intrinsically harder to measure there.**

## 6. Aspiration and feeding (see §6 of [supportive-and-palliative-care.md](supportive-and-palliative-care.md))

### 6.1 The risk ranking needs correcting
Across three feline laryngeal-mass cohorts (partial laryngectomy, 6 cases; temporary tracheostomy, 24 cases; arytenoidectomy, 8 cases), **the dominant signs were stridor and dyspnoea, the principal causes of death were airway obstruction and the primary disease, and in no case was aspiration pneumonia the main thread.**
> **∴ For this class of case, airway management should take priority over aspiration prevention** — but the two are **coupled during feeding.**

⚠️ The frequently cited "laryngeal disease accounted for only 1/33 in the largest feline aspiration pneumonia cohort" **cannot** support the claim that laryngeal dysfunction is a rare cause of aspiration in cats: that study listed **a diagnosis of neoplasia as an explicit exclusion criterion** (19 cats were excluded for neoplasia), and its case definition is **circular**.

### 6.2 The **nature** of the voice loss is the key unresolved question, and it is clinically distinguishable
| If it is | Consequence |
|---|---|
| **Neurogenic** | **Both laryngeal sensation and closure are impaired** → silent aspiration (human data: **72% of aspirators do not cough**) plus reduced cough clearance |
| **Purely mechanical** | Protective reflexes may be preserved |

**Specific questions to put to the attending clinician**: is the nictitating membrane protrusion accompanied by miosis/ptosis? Can laryngoscopy be done to assess arytenoid motion directly?

### 6.3 Stridor while eating = feeding is the high-risk period
Human measurements show strong co-occurrence of stridor with dysphagia (36% vs 11%) and dysphonia (82% vs 23%).
→ Someone should **be present during feeding with an oxygen cage ready**. ⚠️ The value of this advice lies mainly in **responding to an obstructive emergency**, not in preventing aspiration.

⚠️ **The 7–24% aspiration pneumonia rate after canine laryngeal paralysis surgery must not be applied to unoperated cases** (unilateral arytenoid lateralisation artificially destroys glottic closure, so the direction of effect differs). **No percentage figure for aspiration risk can be given; any number would be fabricated.**

## 7. Representative claims judged OVERSTATED in this verification round (cautionary record)

| Original claim | Problem | Direction of correction |
|---|---|---|
| "16 feline HRI cases, 100% environmental" | 10/16 had no trigger recorded | "Among the only 6 with a recorded trigger, all were environmental" |
| "Horner's and otitis media, P<0.01" | The P<0.01 in the abstract belongs to an **age association in dogs** and has nothing to do with otitis | Delete the P value |
| "Cats have lower aspiration risk than dogs because of a more robust laryngospasm" | The source is an **unverified speculation** in the **introduction**, doubly hedged with "appear"/"perhaps", and it forms a **self-citation loop** | Downgrade to "expert opinion (lowest tier)" |
| "75% threshold for subglottic stenosis" | That threshold applies only to **voice**, **not to respiration or airway resistance**; for respiration there is no safe zone "below 75%" | State separately by endpoint |
| "b=-0.85~-1.07, k=2.25" | **Fitted values from a CFD numerical simulation**, not measurements; n=11 humans, no dogs, no cats | Label as a computational model |
| "Advanced age / brachycephaly are the dominant risk factors for environmental HRI" | The authors themselves state that this model had **the least information content** (R²=0.039) | Call them "risk markers", not dominant factors |
