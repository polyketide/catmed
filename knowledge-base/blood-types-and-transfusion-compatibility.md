# Feline Blood Types and Transfusion Compatibility — why an untyped transfusion can kill on the first unit

> Generated 2026-07-23 · PubMed search + verbatim verification · Not diagnostic, not a prescription
> **Purpose**: this file is the evidence base for any question about giving a cat blood — "can any healthy cat donate?", "do we need to type first?", "the group says just transfuse". It exists because the single most dangerous piece of advice that circulates in owner communities about anaemic cats is that blood is blood. In cats, it is not.
> **Why it earns a file of its own**: unlike a dog, a cat can die from its *first* transfusion. The reason is a species fact, not a dosing subtlety, and it is exactly the kind of decision-moment fact an owner is least likely to be handed.

---

## 0. The finding that matters most

### 0.1 A cat can have a fatal reaction to blood it has never seen before

Dogs rarely react badly to a first, un-crossmatched transfusion because they have few clinically significant pre-formed antibodies. **Cats are the opposite.** They carry **naturally occurring alloantibodies in plasma** from birth, so *"donor and recipient should be blood-typed and/or crossmatched before the first transfusion takes place"* and *"mismatched transfusions are ineffective and may cause life-threatening hemolytic transfusion reactions"* (Giger 1992, PMID 1472771).

> **⭐ This is the fact an owner community is deciding without.** "Any healthy cat can give blood" is true for the donor's safety and false for the recipient's. A type A unit into a type B cat is not a weak transfusion — it is an **acute haemolytic reaction that can kill during the bag**. The medicine to prevent it (a typing card, minutes, a few dollars) is trivial; what is usually missing at the decision moment is only the knowledge that it is needed.

### 0.2 The AB system, and why type B cats are the ones who die

The **AB system is the only recognized feline blood group system**, with three types: *"type A is most common, type B is frequently found in certain breeds, and type AB occurs rarely"*, and the frequency *"varies geographically among domestic shorthair cats and between breeds"* (Griot-Wenk & Giger 1995, PMID 8619268). Because of the pre-formed antibodies, *"only AB-matched transfusions are effective and safe."*

The asymmetry that kills is on the **type B** side: *"Owing to the general presence of strong anti-A alloantibodies in type B cats, type A blood given to a type B cat results in life-threatening acute hemolytic transfusion reactions"* (Griot-Wenk & Giger 1995). This is not a rare idiosyncrasy — in a 312-cat survey, **every type B cat tested had agglutinating anti-A antibody**, titres 2 to 256, and the modelled relative risk of an acute *severe* reaction when type A/AB blood reached a type B cat was **6.4%**, with acute mild reactions in most of the rest (Gurkan 2005, PMID 15914059). Type B is uncommon overall but concentrated in certain breeds — which is precisely why "the donor looked healthy" tells you nothing about whether the unit is safe.

### 0.3 The part the textbooks from the 1990s do **not** contain

Here the older reviews stop, and this is the hole a fact-check has to fill. *"Only AB-matched transfusions are effective and safe"* (1995) is now **necessary but not sufficient**:

- **A cat can react to an AB-matched unit.** A separate red-cell antigen, **Mik**, was identified in cats: *"The absence of this novel Mik red cell antigen can be associated with naturally occurring anti-Mik alloantibodies and can elicit an acute hemolytic transfusion reaction after an AB-matched blood transfusion"* (Weinstein 2007, PMID 17427390).
- **Non-AB incompatibility is common enough to matter on the first transfusion.** In 300 cats, major crossmatch incompatibilities appeared in **23 of 154 transfusion-naive cats (14.9%)** — cats that had *never* been transfused — rising to 27% in previously transfused cats (McClosky 2018, PMID 30307648). The authors' conclusion: the prevalence *"is sufficiently high to justify the recommendation to perform a crossmatch before all (including the first) RBC transfusions in cats."*
- **The current standard of care says so.** The **2021 ISFM Consensus Guidelines** are built to give general practitioners *"a practical guide to blood typing, cross-matching, and blood collection and administration"*, and state plainly that *"Cats have naturally occurring alloantibodies to red cell antigens and severe reactions can occur with type-mismatched transfusions"* (Taylor 2021, PMID 33896248).

> **⭐ The currency lesson, made concrete.** Matching a 1995 review would have told you "type first." Only reading *forward* from it — Mik (2007), the crossmatch-prevalence study (2018), the ISFM guideline (2021) — gets you to today's answer: **type AND crossmatch, before the first unit.** "Agrees with the old source" is not the same as "current"; a source's date is part of its evidence.

## 1. What this means at the decision moment

- **Never accept "any cat can donate, just transfuse."** For the recipient, the safe sequence is **blood type the recipient → type-matched donor → crossmatch (major) before the unit runs**, including a first-ever transfusion. Typing and crossmatch kits are commercially available (Griot-Wenk & Giger 1995; Taylor 2021).
- **If a reaction begins, the response is time-critical and simple to start:** *"Immediate withdrawal of a transfusion and supportive care may save a patient"* (Griot-Wenk & Giger 1995). Signs of an acute haemolytic reaction during or just after a unit — collapse, fever, tachypnoea, pigmenturia — are a stop-the-bag emergency for the attending clinician.
- **Type AB recipients are the tolerant exception, not the rule.** Transfusion of type A or B blood to type AB cats produced *"no apparent clinical transfusion reactions"* (Gurkan 2005) — but AB is rare, and this is a reason to type, not to skip typing.
- **This file does not cover** neonatal isoerythrolysis (type A/AB kittens of type B queens), donor infectious-disease screening, or dosing/volume of a transfusion. Those are real and belong to the attending clinician and, for screening, to the ISFM guideline (Taylor 2021). What this file governs is the one claim owners get wrong: that untyped feline blood is safe.
